6.1Writing & Graphing Inequalities ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: You already know what an equation looks like: two expressions set equal. An inequality is similar, but instead of saying two sides are equal, it says one side is greater than, less than, or “at least” or “at most” the other. Don't let it freak you out — it's just a bigger version of the same idea.

Key concept — inequality symbols
<  less than
>  greater than
≤  less than or equal to (closed dot on graph)
≥  greater than or equal to (closed dot on graph)

Alligator trick: The alligator always eats the bigger number — its mouth opens toward the larger side.
Read backwards: When x is on the right (e.g., 4 < x), flip and read “x is greater than 4.” That way you're always saying what x is.

Graphing inequalities on a number line

Chart for graphing inequalities
Strict (< or >): Open circle — the endpoint is NOT included
Non-strict (≤ or ≥): Closed (filled) circle — the endpoint IS included
Greater than: arrow points right  →
Less than: arrow points left  ←

Worked examples

Example 1 — graph x > 3
x > 3 → open circle at 3, arrow pointing right “x is greater than 3”
Example 2 — graph x ≤ −1
x ≤ −1 → closed circle at −1, arrow pointing left “x is less than or equal to negative one”
Example 3 — write inequality from graph (open circle at 2, arrow right)
Open circle = strict. Arrow right = greater than. → x > 2
Joke: How do you make seven even? Take away the “s”.
6.2Solving Basic Inequalities ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: Solving an inequality is nearly identical to solving an equation. Same undo table, same steps — with one critical exception.

The ONE rule that’s different
When you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, you must flip the inequality sign.

Why? Think about it: 2 < 8 is true. Multiply both sides by −1: −2 < −8? No! −2 > −8. The relationship flipped.

Say it out loud every single time you divide or multiply by a negative: “flip the sign.”

Worked example 1 — solve and graph: 3x − 6 < 9

Worked example — positive coefficient (no flip)
3x − 6 < 9       +6    +6       3x < 15       ÷3    ÷3        x < 5  ✓ Graph: open circle at 5, arrow left

Worked example 2 — solve and graph: −4x ≥ 20

Worked example — NEGATIVE coefficient (FLIP!)
−4x ≥ 20 ÷(−4)  ÷(−4)  ← FLIP THE SIGN!      x ≤ −5  ✓ Graph: closed circle at −5, arrow left

Worked example 3 — solve and graph: x/(−2) ≤ 3

Worked example — dividing by negative (FLIP!)
x/(−2) ≤ 3 ×(−2)  ×(−2)  ← FLIP!        x ≥ −6  ✓ Graph: closed circle at −6, arrow right
When do you flip?
Only when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number. Adding or subtracting a negative does NOT require a flip.
Joke: What is a butterfly's favorite subject? Mothematics.
6.3Solving More Complex Inequalities ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: Now we bring in parentheses, like terms, and variables on both sides — just like Topic 5.3 for equations. The same 4-step process applies, with the added flip rule from 6.2.

Process for solving inequalities (4 steps)
Step 1 — Simplify each side: Distribute & combine like terms
Step 2 — Get x on one side: Move the smaller coefficient
Step 3 — Undo: Use the undo steps (and flip if you multiply/divide by negative!)
Step 4 — Graph the solution on a number line

Worked example 1 — solve: 2(x + 3) ≤ 4x − 2

Worked example
2(x + 3) ≤ 4x − 2 2x + 6 ≤ 4x − 2 ← distribute −2x      −2x ← move smaller coeff 6 ≤ 2x − 2 +2         +2 8 ≤ 2x → x ≥ 4 ← read backwards Graph: closed circle at 4, arrow right

Worked example 2 — solve: −3(x − 2) > 12

Worked example — negative distribution, then divide by negative
−3(x − 2) > 12 −3x + 6 > 12 ← circle the −3! distribute         −6    −6      −3x > 6 ÷(−3)  ÷(−3)  ← FLIP!        x < −2  ✓

Worked example 3 — solve: 5x + 1 > 3x − 7

Worked example
5x + 1 > 3x − 7 −3x       −3x 2x + 1 > −7      −1    −1     2x > −8     ÷2   ÷2      x > −4  ✓
6.4Special Case Inequalities ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: In Unit 5 we saw two special cases when solving equations: No Solution and All Real Numbers. The same thing can happen with inequalities!

Special case rules for inequalities
When x cancels out in Step 2:
FALSE statement (e.g., 3 > 7) → No Solution
TRUE statement (e.g., 5 < 9) → All Real Numbers

Same rule as equations — just check if the leftover statement is true or false.

Worked example 1 — No Solution: solve 3x + 5 > 3x + 8

Worked example
3x + 5 > 3x + 8 −3x       −3x 5 > 8 ← FALSE → No Solution No value of x makes 5 > 8 true.

Worked example 2 — All Real Numbers: solve 2x − 1 < 2x + 4

Worked example
2x − 1 < 2x + 4 −2x      −2x −1 < 4 ← TRUE → All Real Numbers Every number x makes this true.
Joke: Why is the equal sign so humble? Because she knows she's not greater than or less than anyone else.
6.5Compound Inequalities ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: A compound inequality is two inequalities joined by and or or. Think about driving: you must go at least 45 mph but no more than 65 mph. That's a compound inequality: 45 ≤ speed ≤ 65.

Two types of compound inequalities
“And” (intersection): Both conditions must be true. Graph = overlap of both graphs.
  Example: x > −2 AND x ≤ 4 → written compactly as −2 < x ≤ 4

“Or” (union): At least one condition is true. Graph = both graphs combined.
  Example: x < −3 OR x ≥ 1

The “In-Between” problem — solve: −2 ≤ 3x + 1 < 10

Key principle for “in-between” problems
Whatever you do to one side, you do to ALL THREE sides!
Worked example — in-between problem
−2 ≤ 3x + 1 < 10 −1       −1  −1 ← subtract 1 from all 3 parts −3 ≤ 3x < 9 ÷3    ÷3  ÷3 −1 ≤ x < 3  ✓ Graph: closed circle at −1, open circle at 3, shaded between

Worked example — “Or” compound: solve 2x + 1 < −5 OR 3x − 2 ≥ 7

Worked example — solve each separately
Left: 2x + 1 < −5 → 2x < −6 → x < −3 Right: 3x − 2 ≥ 7 → 3x ≥ 9 → x ≥ 3 Solution: x < −3 OR x ≥ 3
