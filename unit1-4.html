1.2Adding & Subtracting Integers ▼

An integer is any whole number, positive, negative, or zero. Adding and subtracting integers is all about direction on the number line.

Types of numbers — quick reference
Natural numbers: 1, 2, 3, … (counting numbers)
Whole numbers: 0, 1, 2, 3, … (natural + zero)
Integers: …, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, … (whole + negatives)
Rational numbers: any number writable as a fraction (includes integers, fractions, terminating/repeating decimals)
Key rules
Add positive → move RIGHT on the number line
Add negative → move LEFT on the number line
Subtract → flip the sign and add: a − b = a + (−b)

Tug-o-war method: same signs add, different signs subtract (keep the sign of the bigger absolute value)
Money analogy: positive = money in your pocket, negative = debt
6 Tips & Tricks for integer addition & subtraction
1. Flipping a subtraction: a − b = a + (−b). Subtraction is just adding a negative.
2. Subtracting vs. adding a negative: 5 − 3 and 5 + (−3) give the same result — they mean the same thing!
3. Subtracting a negative: a − (−b) = a + b. Two negatives flip to a positive. (−)(−) = +
4. Adding two negatives: (−a) + (−b) = −(a + b). Same signs → add & keep the negative.
5. Tug-o-war: different signs? The bigger absolute value wins; subtract the smaller and keep that sign.
6. Think money: positive = money you have, negative = debt. −3 + 8 means you owe $3 but have $8 → $5 ahead.

Worked examples

Example 1 — −2 + 5
Start at −2, move right 5 spaces −2 + 5 = 3
Example 2 — −4 + (−7)
Same signs → add: 4 + 7 = 11, keep negative −4 + (−7) = −11
Example 3 — 3 − (−6)
Flip: 3 − (−6) = 3 + 6 = 9
Number Line: −3 + 5 = 2
Number Line: −4 + (−3) = −7  (negative + negative)

There’s no single right technique — pick what works for you on each problem.

1.3Multiplying & Dividing Integers ▼

The key rule for multiplying and dividing integers is all about the signs. Let’s discover why by looking at a pattern.

Discover the pattern — why does positive × negative = negative?
Watch what happens as the second factor decreases by 1:
3 ×  2 =  6decreasing by 3 each time ↓
3 ×  1 =  3
3 ×  0 =  0
3 × (−1) = −3← keeps decreasing by 3
3 × (−2) = −6← pattern forces a negative
Notice the pattern → positive × negative = negative.
Now extend the same logic to negative × negative: if 3 × (−2) = −6, then (−3) × (−2) must flip the sign again → negative × negative = positive.
Sign rules
Same signs → positive answer
   (+)(+) = +    (−)(−) = +

Different signs → negative answer
   (+)(−) = −    (−)(+) = −

The same rules apply to division.

Worked examples

Example 1
(−4) × (−5) = +20 ← same signs = positive
Example 2
3 × (−7) = −21 ← different signs = negative
Example 3
(−18) ÷ (−3) = +6 ← same signs = positive
1.4Order of Operations ▼

A numerical expression is a combination of numbers and operations. To evaluate it means to find its value. We always follow PEMDAS.

PEMDAS — Order of Operations
P — Parentheses (innermost first)
E — Exponents
M/D — Multiply & Divide (left to right)
A/S — Add & Subtract (left to right)

Memory trick: “Please Excuse My Dear Aunt Sally”

Operation vocabulary: Adding → sum  •  Subtracting → difference  •  Multiplying → product  •  Dividing → quotient

Worked examples

Example 1 — Evaluate: 3 + 4 × 2
Multiply first: 4 × 2 = 8 Then add: 3 + 8 = 11
Example 2 — Evaluate: (3 + 4) × 2
Parentheses first: 3 + 4 = 7 Then multiply: 7 × 2 = 14
Example 3 — Evaluate: 2² + 3 × (5 − 2)
P: (5 − 2) = 3 → 2² + 3 × 3 E: 2² = 4 → 4 + 3 × 3 M: 3 × 3 = 9 → 4 + 9 = 13
2.1Introduction to Fractions ▼

A fraction represents part of a whole — like slicing a pizza into equal pieces: the denominator is how many slices, the numerator is how many you take. The numerator (top) tells how many parts you have. The denominator (bottom) tells how many equal parts the whole is split into.

Three forms of a negative fraction (all equivalent)
−ab  =  a−b  =  −ab

The first form (−ab) is preferred.
Example 1
34 means: 3 out of 4 equal parts
Example 2 — Equivalent fractions
12 = 24 = 36 = 48 (all the same value)
Example 3 — Negative fraction
−34 = 3−4 = −34 ← all equivalent; use −34
Fraction Model: 3/4 shaded
Number Line: fractions between 0 and 2
2.3Simplifying Fractions ▼

To simplify a fraction, divide both the numerator and denominator by their GCF (Greatest Common Factor). The value of the fraction does not change — you just write it in a simpler form.

GCF Method
Step 1 — Find the GCF of numerator and denominator
Step 2 — Divide both by the GCF
Step 3 — Check: can it simplify further?
Example 1 — Simplify 812
GCF(8,12) = 4 8 ÷ 4 = 2,  12 ÷ 4 = 3 = 23
Example 2 — Simplify 1525
GCF(15,25) = 5 → 15/25 = 35
Example 3 — Simplify 1824
GCF(18,24) = 6 → 18/24 = 34
Alternatively: factor & cancel
You can also factor the numerator and denominator, then cancel common factors — same result as using the GCF.
Example: 1218 → 2 × 63 × 6 → 23  (the 6s cancel)
2.4Adding & Subtracting Fractions ▼
Key insight
You can only add parts that are the same size. That’s why the denominators must match — the denominator tells you the size of each piece. You can’t add thirds to quarters until they’re the same piece size.

Same denominator: add or subtract the numerators only; keep the denominator. Different denominators: find the LCD (Least Common Denominator), convert each fraction to an equivalent fraction with that denominator, then add or subtract. Always simplify the answer.

Example 1 — Same denominator: 38 + 18
Same denominator → add numerators: 3 + 1 = 4 = 48 = 12
Example 2 — Different denominators: 14 + 13
LCD of 4 and 3 = 12 14 = 312    13 = 412 312 + 412 = 712
Example 3 — Subtraction: 56 − 14
LCD of 6 and 4 = 12 56 = 1012    14 = 312 1012 − 312 = 712
Common Denominator: 1/4 + 1/3 → 3/12 + 4/12
2.5Multiplying & Dividing Fractions ▼

Multiply: multiply numerators together, multiply denominators together, then simplify. Divide: use Keep-Change-Flip (KCF) — keep the first fraction, change ÷ to ×, flip the second fraction, then multiply.

Keep-Change-Flip (KCF)
ab ÷ cd  →  Keep ab  ×  Flip dc
Example 1 — Multiply: 23 × 34
Numerators: 2 × 3 = 6    Denominators: 3 × 4 = 12 = 612 = 12
Example 2 — Divide: 34 ÷ 12
KCF: 34 × 21 = 64 = 32
Example 3 — Divide: 25 ÷ 45
KCF: 25 × 54 = 1020 = 12
Area Model: 2/3 × 3/4 = 6/12 = 1/2
3.1Introduction to Variables ▼

A variable is a symbol (usually x or y) that represents an unknown number. Mr. Behling’s rule: “A variable is your friend — just a number. Don’t let it freak you out.”

Whatever you can do to a number, you can do to a variable. Add it, subtract it, multiply it, divide it — same rules, just with a letter standing in for the number.

Key ideas about variables
Multiplication shortcut: 2x means “2 times x” — no × symbol needed
Invisible coefficient: x = 1·x and −x = −1·x
Operations with variables look like: x+5, x−3, 4x, x/2
Example 1
x means “some unknown number” If x = 5, then x + 3 = 5 + 3 = 8
Example 2 — Invisible coefficient
x = 1x    −x = −1x
Example 3 — Reading expressions
4x = 4 times x    x/2 = x divided by 2
3.2Adding & Subtracting Variables (Like Terms) ▼

Like terms have the same variable with the same exponent (or are both constants). You can only combine like terms — you cannot combine unlike terms.

The apple analogy
3 apples + 1 apple = 4 apples
3a + 1a = 4a    (same idea!)

3 apples + 1 banana ≠ 4 anything → cannot combine unlike terms
Example 1
3x + 5x = 8x ← like terms, add coefficients
Example 2
7y − 3y + 2y = 6y
Example 3 — Unlike terms
3x + 5 → cannot simplify (x-term and constant are unlike)
3.3Multiplying Variables (Exponents) ▼

When you multiply the same variable repeatedly, use exponents as a shortcut. Mathematicians are lazy — we use shortcuts!

Common mistake — adding vs. multiplying
3x + 3x = 6x  (add coefficients — same idea as 3 apples + 3 apples = 6 apples)
3x · 3x = 9x²  (multiply coefficients AND add exponents)
Don’t mix them up! Adding keeps the same exponent; multiplying changes it.
Exponent shortcut for multiplying
x · x = x²    x · x · x = x³

When multiplying powers with the same base: ADD the exponents
x² · x³ = x&sup5;    x&sup4; · x = x&sup5;

Multiply coefficients separately: (2x³)(4x²) = 8x&sup5;
Example 1
x² · x³ = x(2+3) = x&sup5;
Example 2
(3x²)(5x³) = 15x&sup5; ← multiply coefficients, add exponents
Example 3
(2x³)(4x²) = 8x&sup5;
Reading aloud & order doesn’t matter
Reading exponents: x² = “x squared” or “x to the second power”  •  x³ = “x cubed”  •  x&sup4; = “x to the fourth”
Commutativity: Multiplication can be done in any order — (3x²)(5x³) = (5x³)(3x²) = 15x&sup5;. Pick the order that feels easiest!
4.1The Language of Algebra ▼

Algebra has its own vocabulary. Understanding the parts of an expression is like understanding the parts of a sentence.

Anatomy of an Expression: 3x² + 2x − 9
Equation vs. Expression: 3x + 5 = 11
Key vocabulary
Term: a number, a variable, or a number times a variable (e.g., 3x, −5, x²)
Coefficient: the number in front of the variable (e.g., in 3x, the coefficient is 3)
Constant: a term with no variable (just a number, e.g., 7)
Algebraic expression: one or more terms combined with + or − (no equal sign)
Equation: has an equal sign.  Expression: no equal sign.
Example 1 — Identify parts of 3x + 5
Terms: 3x and 5 Coefficient of x: 3 Constant: 5
Example 2 — Expression or Equation?
2x + 7 → Expression (no = sign) 2x + 7 = 11 → Equation (has = sign)
Example 3 — Identify: −4x² + 2x − 9
3 terms. Coefficients: −4 and 2. Constant: −9
Invisible 1 coefficient
When you see just “x” with no number in front, the coefficient is 1 (it’s invisible). So x = 1x, −x = −1x, and x² = 1x². Never forget the hidden 1!
4.2Simplifying Expressions ▼

To simplify an expression: (1) Distribute to remove parentheses, (2) Combine like terms. Say it out loud when distributing!

Distributive Property: 3(x + 4)
Distributive Property
a(b + c) = ab + ac

Draw arcs from the outside term to each inside term. Say it out loud!

Watch the negative! Circle the negative sign before parentheses.
−(x + 3) = −1(x + 3) = −x − 3
Example 1 — Simplify: 3(x + 4) − 2x
Distribute: 3x + 12 − 2x Combine like terms: x + 12
Example 2 — Simplify: −(x + 3) + 5x
Circle the negative: −1(x + 3) + 5x Distribute: −x − 3 + 5x Combine: 4x − 3
Example 3 — Simplify: 2(3x − 1) + 4
Distribute: 6x − 2 + 4 Combine constants: 6x + 2
Standard ordering rule
Write your final answer from highest to lowest exponent, with the constant last. Example: 3x² + 2x − 5. This is called standard form for a polynomial.
4.4Evaluating Expressions ▼

To evaluate an expression, replace the variable with the given number (use parentheses!), then use PEMDAS. Remember: “A variable is just a number. Don’t let it freak you out.”

Process
Step 1 — Substitute (replace x with the given number, put in parentheses)
Step 2 — Use PEMDAS to evaluate
Example 1 — Evaluate 3x − 5 when x = 4
3(4) − 5 = 12 − 5 = 7
Example 2 — Evaluate 3x − 5 when x = −2
3(−2) − 5 = −6 − 5 = −11
Example 3 — Evaluate 3x − 5 when x = 0
3(0) − 5 = 0 − 5 = −5
