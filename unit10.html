10.1Translating Words to Algebra ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: Algebra is just a language. Before you can solve a word problem, you must translate the English into math. Certain key words always map to the same operations.

Key Words → Operations
Sum, plus, more than, increased by, added to:  +
Difference, less than, decreased by, subtracted from:  −
Product, times, twice (2×), double:  ×
Quotient, divided by, ratio of:  ÷
Is, equals, gives, results in:  =
A number, some number, an unknown:  x (or any variable)
Watch out: "less than" reverses order!
"5 less than a number" means x − 5, NOT 5 − x. The phrase names what comes first in the subtraction.
4-Step Problem-Solving Process
Step 1 — Define variable: Let x = ...
Step 2 — Write equation from the words
Step 3 — Solve the equation
Step 4 — Answer the question in context (with units!)

Worked example — "The difference of five times a number and two is 13. Find the number."

Worked example (from Semester 1 Final)
Let x = the number 5x − 2 = 13 5x = 15 x = 3 ✓

10.2One-Variable Word Problems ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: Now you apply the 4-step process to number problems, age problems, and consecutive integer problems. Always define your variable first!

Types of problems in 10.2
Number problems: "Two more than three times a number is 11."
Age problems: "In 5 years, Maria will be twice as old as Tom."
Consecutive integers: n, n+1, n+2 (consecutive) or n, n+2, n+4 (consecutive odd/even)

Worked example — consecutive integers

Worked example
Find 3 consecutive integers that sum to 48. Let n = first integer n + (n+1) + (n+2) = 48 3n + 3 = 48 3n = 45 n = 15 → integers: 15, 16, 17 ✓

Worked example — from Semester 1 Final

Worked example (Gary's Garage)
Total cost $1060. Labor $140/hr. Parts $500. Let x = number of hours 140x + 500 = 1060 140x = 560 x = 4 hours ✓
10.3Rate, Time & Distance Problems ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: These problems involve movement. The key formula connects distance, rate (speed), and time.

The d = r × t Formula
d = r × t   distance = rate × time

Rearranged:   r = d/t    t = d/r
Three Situations
Same direction: difference in distances
Opposite directions: distances add up to total
Toward each other: distances add up; sum = total distance

Worked example — opposite directions

Worked example
Two cars leave at same time in opposite directions. Car A: 60 mph. Car B: 45 mph. After how many hours are they 315 miles apart? Let t = time 60t + 45t = 315 105t = 315 t = 3 hours ✓

Worked example — one object

Worked example
A train travels 240 miles in 4 hours. What is its speed? r = d/t = 240/4 = 60 mph ✓
10.4Mixture & Multi-Step Problems ▼

Connect to prior knowledge: Mixture problems combine two things (quantities, concentrations, prices) to find an unknown. They use the idea of weighted averages.

System approach for mixture problems
Let x = quantity of item A
Then total − x = quantity of item B
Set up: (value of A) + (value of B) = (total value)

Worked example — from Semester 1 Final (shopping problem)

Worked example
Jeans cost $42 each. Shirts cost $12 each. Bought 5 items. Spent $126. Let j = jeans. Then 5 − j = shirts. 42j + 12(5 − j) = 126 42j + 60 − 12j = 126 30j = 66 j = 2.2... → j = 2 jeans, 3 shirts ✓
Multi-Step Strategy
Break the problem into parts. Identify what is unknown, what relationships exist, and what equation captures those relationships. Don't rush — read twice.

Example
